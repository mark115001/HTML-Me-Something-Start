ALASKA VOYAGE

May 2023

The Ruby Princess

Set sale on the Ruby Princess from San Fransciso Bay, Ca., Pier 27. This was the beginning of three days of sailing before reaching the first port in Skagway, Alaska. However, four hours into the cruise, a passenger medical emergency occurred and the ship had to return to San Fransicso where the passenger was picked up by the US Coast Guard.I believe I heard the passenger was OK. Since sailing to Skagway was 8 hours behind, the ship could not reach the port as scheduled so we headed for what was supposed to be our third destination. It was not a port. It was a Glacier in the Endicott Arm inlet.

Endicott Arm-The Dawes Glacier

Sailing into Endicott Arm inlet to reach the Dawes Glacier took more than an hour. We were sailing in between mountains through the very deep fjords that were created by glaciers over the millennia of time. The water was completely calm except for what the ship was creating. The picture to the left is the Dawes Glacier. This glacier has been losing water and that can be seen in the small chunks of ices that are shown floating away. The most interesting part was that the ship's captain wanted to give all three thousand-plus passengers a good view of the glacier so he maneuvered the ship into the middle of the fjord and then spun the ship 360 degrees so every passenger had great view of the glacier and could get all the pictures they wanted. Then we traveled out of Endicott Arm and on to the first port in Skagway, Alaska.

Port of Skagway Alaska

We ate lunch on the first floor of the Red Onion bar and grill. This bar has been around since the gold rush days and while it's always been a bar or saloon, downstairs,upstairs are where the "ladies of the evening" worked. The waitresses were dressed like the "ladies" used to be when then upstairs was not just a showplace like it is today. At any rate, the food was good and the place was absolutely packed, likely because of the ship but also there were not many places to eat. The main thing we did in Skagway was to go see where they trained the Iditarod sled dogs. There was obviously no snow but they still pulled sleds and learned to work together. We took a sled ride and then got to meet and pet the dogs that pulled our sled. Then we went and looked at the puppies that would eventually become sled dogs. Before going back to the ship we walked down the main street bought some kettle corn and headed back to the ship. Next stop Juneau, Alaksa.

Juneau, Alaska-Mendenhall Glacier

We had enough time in Juneau to do two things. Right after we arrived, we got on a bus to head out on another boat to look for humpback whales. That's actually why I alwayswanted to take a cruise to Alaska, to see whales. I didn't know really anything else to do in Alaska but I wanted to see whales so we signed up for an excusion out into to thelocal Juneau waters with a guarentee to either see whales or get our money back. There were about 30 to 40 people on this smaller boat and we sailed out about 15 to 20 minutes from shore and then kind of just stopped. The captain announced he had seen whales on the starboard(right) side and we all started looking. It's really funny to see 30 or 40 people move to one side of the boat at the same time but the captain was correct, we saw a cloud of air forcefully expelled. The captain said we found a group of about 10 whales of all ages but you really can't tell how big a whale your looking at is with just a blowhole but no matter what age it was, we got to see what we paid for. We even saw one whale divebreach and we saw its dorsal fins but it was just one time. After that successful excursion, we were driven to another glacier. The Mendenhall Glacier. This glacier was much bigger than the Dawes Glacier that we sway a couple of days earlier but as you can see in the picture to the left, a large piece had broken off recently and left the rock exposed. There was one other amazing feature at this glacier and that was a huge waterfall from the melting snow from the end of the winter season. After this we were bussed back to the Ruby Princess and we set off for our next stop, Ketchikan, Alaska.

Ketchikan, Alaska

Right after arrival, we attended a lumberjack show. It was absolutely amazing to watch the competion on 5 differnt challenges. Pole climbing, wood chopping, ax throwing, log rolling and I don't recall the fifth one. These were skills needed when logging was still occurring in Alaska and Canada. Logging was very dangerous and it was evident with the tests they were doing. Learning about Skagway on a small boat cruise was also amazing. First we were able to see up close a pair of blad eagles. Their nest was huge. The boat captain said the "couple", who almost always mate for life, add on to the nest each year. The cruise went past a row of houses that were next to the lake. We were told there was no running water in this area and that all water for bathing and cleaning came from rain water. As a result, every house not only had a boat but they also had a storage tank tocatch the rain. There is tens of feet of rain in this region yearly. Next, we say a house with totem poles where native Tlingit families lived. There were mutiple generations in one house that rarely had more than a few rooms and everyone in the family lived there. The families also built totem poles that told about the family that lived there. Most of these houses were no longer in use but the totem poles were still there. Then we found out that all children not only learn how to drive a boat when they are very young so they can take themselves to school but they have to go on supervised survival training when they are in their teens. They are only given one item, can't recall what that was, but everything they needed to live and start a fire needed to be in that one item. Next we headed on to the final destination, the only Canada stop, Prince Rupert, British Columbia.

Prince Rupert, British Columbia

We were only at this port for about 3 hours. We had to have passports with us when we left the ship although we were never asked for it while there. The first thing we saw was an RCMP, Royal Canadian Mounted Policeman in full red and black uniform and hat, just like I've always seen on TV. He posed for pictures with passengers. The big thing at this port was I needed to buy a new razor. Mine broke and we were still more than three days from San Francisco so I needed to purchase a new one. Fortunately, after speaking with an very kind lady about Prince Rupert and Canadian health care, she pointed up to a WalMart where I was able to purchase a new razor. Once back on the ship, we settled in for the final three days of sailing before arriving back in San Francisco, Pier 27.

The Ruby Princess-Voyage Home

Back aboard the ship, we did the same things we did for the first three days of the cruise, eat and try to find some shows that passed the time. Honestly, there was not really enough sailing time for me as I marveled at the sunsets and the vastness of the ocean which I new was the case but could never really put it into perspective until now. I was glad to be headed home but I could not and still can't get over Alaska. I truly want to go back and visit one of the ports we were at, probably Juneau first, but there are also other port cities that were not on this cruise that I like to see sometime although anywhere in Alaska where I could get up every day and see the beauty of the mountains or the ocean would be amazing. I never would have done this myself but I have to give my daughter another huge thank you for taking me on what has turned out to be the best vacation I've ever experienced.
